## Post 2: The Surprising Data Point

The #1 reason engineers fail interviews isn't DSA.

It's behavioral rounds.

I've talked to 50+ engineers who went through FAANG interviews in the past year.

The pattern is clear:

- 90% prepared for coding rounds
- 60% prepared for system design
- Less than 20% prepared for behavioral

But here's the thing:

Amazon rejects ~40% of candidates who pass all technical rounds — in the behavioral round alone.

Google calls it "Googleyness." Meta calls it "Culture fit." Amazon has 16 Leadership Principles.

Different names. Same reality: they're testing if you can communicate, take ownership, and learn from failure.

The fix is simple:

1. Write down 8-10 stories from your career
2. Structure each one: Situation, Task, Action, Result
3. Practice saying them out loud (not just reading them)
4. Time yourself — 2-3 minutes per answer

I've compiled 50 behavioral questions with STAR-format answers at guru-sishya.in — free to use, no login needed.

But honestly? Even if you don't use my resource — just prepare your stories. It's the highest-ROI interview prep activity you can do.

## Post 4: The Tip Post

System design interviews: the 5-minute trick that changed everything for me.

Most candidates start drawing boxes immediately.

Top candidates spend the first 5 minutes asking questions.

Here's my exact opening for any system design question:

"Before I start designing, let me clarify a few things:"

1. Who are the users? (consumers, businesses, internal)
2. What's the expected scale? (DAU, QPS, data volume)
3. What are the latency requirements? (real-time, near-real-time, batch)
4. Availability vs consistency — which matters more?
5. Any specific constraints? (budget, existing infra, compliance)

This does three things:

- Shows you think before you code
- Narrows the problem to a manageable scope
- Gives you concrete numbers for capacity estimation

I've never seen a candidate fail after a strong requirements gathering phase.

I've seen many fail who jumped straight to "So I'd use Kafka and Redis..."

Want to practice this? I have structured system design sessions with case studies at guru-sishya.in — each one starts with requirements gathering exercises.

## Post 5: The Myth-Busting Post

"You need to solve 500+ LeetCode problems to crack FAANG."

This is a myth. And it's burning people out.

Here's what actually matters:

Pattern recognition > Problem count

There are roughly 15-20 core patterns in DSA:
- Two pointers
- Sliding window
- Binary search variations
- BFS/DFS
- Dynamic programming (knapsack, LCS, LIS)
- Graph algorithms (shortest path, topological sort)
- Tree traversals
- Stack/queue patterns
- Heap/priority queue
- Union-Find
- Backtracking
- Greedy

If you deeply understand these patterns, you can solve new problems you've never seen.

If you've "solved" 500 problems by looking at solutions after 5 minutes, you can't solve anything new.

Quality over quantity. Every time.

My recommendation:
- 3-4 problems per pattern
- Understand WHY the pattern works (not just HOW)
- Code the solution without looking at hints
- Revisit after 3 days to check retention

I've organized 138 topics by pattern at guru-sishya.in — each with structured sessions, quizzes, and cheatsheets. Free to use.

But the principle applies regardless of what resource you use: learn patterns, not problems.

## Post 7: The Actionable Tip List

Your 6-week interview prep plan (for working professionals):

Week 1-2: DSA Fundamentals
- Arrays, strings, linked lists
- Trees, graphs, heaps
- 2-3 problems/day (pattern-based, not random)

Week 3: System Design Basics
- Load balancers, caching, databases
- Practice 2 classic designs (URL shortener, chat system)

Week 4: System Design Advanced
- Practice 3 more designs (news feed, rate limiter, notification system)
- Focus on tradeoff discussions

Week 5: Behavioral + Language-Specific
- Write 8-10 STAR stories
- Review Java/Python/JS specifics for your target company

Week 6: Mock Interviews + Revision
- 2-3 mock interviews (coding + system design)
- Cheatsheet revision
- Light practice only (no new topics)

Key rules:
- 2 hours/day max (burnout kills prep quality)
- No new topics in the last week
- Practice explaining out loud (interviews are conversations)

## Post 8: The Hot Take

Unpopular opinion: Most interview prep platforms are designed to keep you subscribed, not to get you hired.

Think about it:

- "2,000+ problems!" (You'll never finish them)
- "New problems added weekly!" (Creating artificial urgency)
- "Premium-only editorials" (The explanation is behind a paywall)
- "Mock interview packages: $99/session" (Fear-based upselling)

The incentive structure is broken.

A platform that actually gets you hired... loses a customer.

A platform that keeps you feeling "almost ready"... retains a subscriber.

This is why I made guru-sishya.in free for core features.

Because interview prep should have an end date.

Prepare. Interview. Get the job. Move on.

Not: Subscribe. Grind. Feel inadequate. Renew. Repeat.

## Post 10: The Contrarian Wisdom Post

Stop studying for interviews. Start preparing.

Studying = passive consumption
- Watching YouTube tutorials
- Reading "Top 50 Interview Questions" articles
- Bookmarking resources you'll never open

Preparing = active practice
- Solving problems with a timer
- Explaining system designs out loud
- Writing your behavioral stories in STAR format
- Doing mock interviews with real feedback

The difference is enormous.

I've seen engineers who "studied" for 3 months fail interviews.

I've seen engineers who actively prepared for 3 weeks get offers.

The secret isn't more time. It's better time.

Active preparation checklist:
- Solve, don't just read solutions
- Draw system designs, don't just watch videos
- Practice speaking, don't just think in your head
- Time yourself, don't practice in comfort mode

This is exactly why I built guru-sishya.in with quizzes, code playgrounds, and timed challenges — not just reading material.

But tools don't matter if the approach is wrong.

Prepare actively. Everything else follows.
